# answer.md — "Tell me about a time you made a technical trade-off" (Primavera)

## Answer (v2 — determinism-only, eng-doc-grounded)
Construction schedules live or die on the end date, and weather is the thing that quietly wrecks it — you lose working days to wind, flooding, frozen ground, and if you've just penciled in a flat "we'll lose a week to rain" buffer, you're planning on a fiction. What this system was actually for was replacing that guess with something defensible: estimate how many non-working days weather will realistically cost a project — month by month, tied to real historical weather for that location and season — and roll that into a project end date and a schedule-confidence number a client can actually stand behind.

And that's where my hard problem sat. Once a client is committing crews and budget to that end date, getting a number isn't enough — it has to be the same number twice. A client pushes back on their date, you re-run it in front of them, and it had better come back identical, or the whole thing loses credibility.

The probabilistic side was owned by our ML engineer, Divanch. His constraint was statistical fidelity: the weather calendar runs a 100-iteration Monte Carlo that turns period-level weather assumptions into day-level non-working-day distributions, and he wanted those distributions as trustworthy as possible.

My side was turning that simulation into a production system: weather periods as first-class backend objects with a CRUD API, decoupled from the risk-analysis execution — reusable — and deterministic, auditable risk numbers coming out the other end — reproducible.

The trade-off sat right at that seam: fidelity versus reproducibility. Richer, unconstrained sampling gives tighter distributions — what Divanch wanted. But an unseeded Monte Carlo draws a fresh random sequence every run, so the same inputs produce a different P80 each time. In a risk-analysis product, that's not a rounding detail — it's a correctness failure.

And the two things at stake were genuinely different failures, both non-negotiable. Divanch's was the number being statistically right — a noisy distribution or a bad persistence value means the P80 is wrong at the source, and a client plans a season against a date that was never achievable. Mine was the number being defendable — because even a correct P80 is useless if a client challenges it in a risk review and we can't regenerate the exact figure. A right number you can't reproduce fails that review just as hard as a reproducible number that's wrong.

So I made them coexist instead of picking one. A seeded RNG meant I kept Divanch's full sampling but the run became reproducible — same inputs, same P80, every time. The seed and the input assumptions were versioned per run, so a date generated months earlier could be regenerated exactly for review. And I put a validation boundary at the interface — persistence between zero and one, no negative durations — so a bad input couldn't quietly corrupt the output.

The reason that validation mattered is the first way this fails — and it's dangerous precisely because it never crashes. Picture a persistence value of 1.3 slipping in from an upstream unit bug. Persistence is a probability; it's meant to cap at one. At 1.3, the simulation treats every bad-weather day as guaranteeing the next, non-working days compound across the season, and the distribution comes out smooth, plausible, and weeks too pessimistic. The client sees a defensible-looking date and plans against garbage — no error, no alert, a wrong number wearing a right one's costume. That's the failure the bounds check stands in front of.

The second would've happened in a room. A client disputes their P80 in a risk review — too conservative, they say. We pull up the exact inputs to walk them through it, re-run, and the number comes back different, because nothing fixed the random sequence. Now we're defending a schedule date we can't even regenerate — and their trust doesn't just drop on that figure, they stop believing every number the module ever produced. That's what the seeded RNG plus versioned assumptions prevent.
